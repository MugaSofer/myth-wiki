## Overview

**Troy**, called **Ilios** or **Ilion** in the sources, is the walled city of Priam on the plain of the Scamander, and the central location of the [Iliad](../sources/iliad.md). Its mythological significance lies especially in its divine connections: its walls were built by gods, its destruction was willed by gods, and it had been sacked once before by Heracles.

## The Walls Built by Gods

### In the Iliad

[Poseidon](../deities/poseidon.md) tells [Apollo](../deities/apollo.md) of their shared history with Troy: "Thou rememberest not all the ills we twain alone of gods endured at Ilios, when by ordinance of Zeus we came to proud Laomedon and served him through a year for promised recompense, and he laid on us his commands. I round their city built the Trojans a wall, wide and most fair, that the city might be unstormed, and thou Phoebus, didst herd shambling crook-horned kine among the spurs of woody many-folded Ida."[^il-wall]

But "then redoubtable Laomedon robbed us of all hire, and sent us off with threats. He threatened that he would bind together our feet and hands and sell us into far-off isles."[^il-cheated]

In Book VII, Poseidon complains that the Achaeans have built their own wall, and "men will forget the wall that I and Phoebus Apollo built with travail for the hero Laomedon."[^il-forget]

### In the Apollodorus

[Apollodorus](../sources/apollodorus.md) gives a different but complementary account: "desiring to put the wantonness of Laomedon to the proof, Apollo and Poseidon assumed the likeness of men and undertook to fortify Pergamum for wages. But when they had fortified it, he would not pay them their wages. Therefore Apollo sent a pestilence, and Poseidon a sea monster."[^apoll-fortify]

## The First Sacking by Heracles

Heracles saved Laomedon's daughter Hesione from the sea monster, "promised to save her on condition of receiving from Laomedon the mares which Zeus had given in compensation for the rape of Ganymede." But Laomedon again refused to pay. Heracles later returned "with eighteen ships of fifty oars each" and sacked the city. "Telamon was the first to breach the wall and enter the city." Heracles "shot down Laomedon and his sons, except Podarces," who was ransomed by Hesione and renamed Priam.[^apoll-heracles]

In the [Iliad](../sources/iliad.md), Tlepolemus recalls that his father Heracles "erst came hither for Laomedon's mares with but six ships and a scantier host, yet sacked the city of Ilios and made her highways desolate."[^il-heracles] Sarpedon replies that Heracles "verily overthrew holy Ilios by the folly of the proud man Laomedon, that rewarded his good deed with harsh upbraiding."[^il-sarpedon]

## Zeus's Regard for Troy

In the [Iliad](../sources/iliad.md), Zeus declares his special affection for the city: "of all cities beneath sun and starry heaven that are the dwelling of mortal men, holy Ilios was most honoured of my heart, and Priam and the folk of Priam of the good ashen spear. For never did mine altar lack the seemly feast, even drink-offering and burnt-offering, the worship that is our due."[^il-zeus]

## The Palladium

[Apollodorus](../sources/apollodorus.md) relates that when Ilus founded Ilium, "having prayed to Zeus that a sign might be shown to him, he beheld by day the [Palladium](../objects/palladium.md), fallen from heaven, lying before his tent. It was three cubits in height, its feet joined together; in its right hand it held a spear aloft, and in the other hand a distaff and spindle."[^apd-palladium]

The Palladium was an image of [Athena](../deities/athena.md)'s childhood friend Pallas, whom she had accidentally killed. [Zeus](../deities/zeus.md) threw it to Troy, and while it remained within the walls, the city could not be taken. It was eventually stolen by [Odysseus](../heroes/odysseus.md) and [Diomedes](../heroes/diomedes.md) with [Helen](../heroes/helen.md)'s help (E.5.13).

## Paris and the Firebrand Dream

"When a second babe was about to be born Hecuba dreamed she had brought forth a firebrand, and that the fire spread over the whole city and burned it." Aesacus declared "the child was begotten to be the ruin of his country and advised that the babe should be exposed." The infant was left on Ida, "nursed for five days by a bear," then raised by a servant who named him Paris.[^apd-paris]

Paris later judged the beauty contest between [Hera](../deities/hera.md), [Athena](../deities/athena.md), and [Aphrodite](../deities/aphrodite.md) (see [Judgment of Paris](../events/judgment-of-paris.md)), choosing Aphrodite's offer of [Helen](../heroes/helen.md), and so brought about the war.

## The Fall of Troy

After ten years of siege, [Odysseus](../heroes/odysseus.md) "invented the construction of the [Wooden Horse](../objects/wooden-horse.md) and suggested it to Epeus." The Greeks hid warriors inside and feigned departure. The Trojans "with great joy dragged the horse, and stationing it beside the palace of [Priam](../heroes/priam.md) deliberated what they should do." Cassandra and Laocoon warned them, but "most were in favour of sparing it as a votive offering sacred to a divinity."[^apd-horse]

By night the Greeks emerged: "they opened the horse and came forth with their arms... having opened the gates they admitted their comrades who had landed from Tenedos." Neoptolemus "slew Priam, who had taken refuge at the altar of Zeus of the Courtyard." [Menelaus](../heroes/menelaus.md) slew Deiphobus and recovered Helen. Aeneas "took up his father Anchises and fled, and the Greeks let him alone on account of his piety." They threw Astyanax from the battlements, slaughtered Polyxena on Achilles' grave, and "having slain the Trojans, they set fire to the city."[^apd-fall]

## In the Epic Cycle

The [Epic Cycle](../sources/epic-cycle.md) fragments describe the final fall of Troy. The Sack of Ilium relates that the Greeks built the wooden horse, and "the Trojans were suspicious of the wooden horse and standing round it debated what they ought to do." The city was taken when the Greeks "sailed in from Tenedos, and those in the wooden horse came out."[^epic-sack]

## The Lineage

[Apollodorus](../sources/apollodorus.md) traces the royal line: Ilus married Eurydice and begat Laomedon, who begat Tithonus, Priam, and others. "After that Ilium was captured by Hercules, ... Podarces, who was called Priam, came to the throne."[^apoll-lineage]
