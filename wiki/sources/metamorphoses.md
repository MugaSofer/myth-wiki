# Ovid, Metamorphoses

| | |
|---|---|
| **Author** | Publius Ovidius Naso (Ovid) |
| **Date** | 8 CE |
| **Translation** | Brookes More (1922), Cornhill Publishing Co. |
| **Type** | Mythological epic poem (15 books, ~12,000 lines) |

## Overview

The **Metamorphoses** is a Latin narrative poem in fifteen books covering myths from the creation of the cosmos to the deification of Julius Caesar, unified by the theme of transformation. It is the single most comprehensive ancient mythological text, encompassing over 250 distinct myths organized in a loosely chronological framework from primordial chaos to contemporary Rome.

Ovid draws primarily on Greek sources but writes for a Roman audience, using Latin divine names throughout. His versions often diverge from earlier Greek traditions — adding psychological depth, ironic detachment, and rhetorical elaboration that make his retellings distinctive. Many myths survive only through Ovid's telling (Narcissus, Pyramus and Thisbe, Pygmalion, Philemon and Baucis, etc.).

## Contents

- **Book I**: Ovid opens with the creation of the cosmos from primordial Chaos — the separation of sea, earth, and sky, and the fashioning of man. The [Four Ages](../events/five-ages.md) follow (Gold, Silver, Bronze, Iron), culminating in cosmic corruption. [Zeus](../deities/zeus.md) summons a divine assembly ("leaning on his ivory scepter, thrice he shook his awful locks, wherewith he moved the earth, and seas and stars") and decides to destroy mankind. The council scene includes Lycaon, king of Arcadia, who tested the god by serving human flesh and was transformed into a wolf. Zeus sends the Great Flood ([Poseidon](../deities/poseidon.md) strikes the earth with his trident, rivers overflow, dolphins dart through oak trees); only [Deucalion](../events/deucalions-flood.md) and Pyrrha survive, adrift on a mountain peak, and repopulate the earth by casting stones behind them. Then [Apollo](../deities/apollo.md) slays the Python and, mocking Cupid's bow, is struck with a golden arrow and falls passionately in love with [Daphne](../heroes/daphne.md); she is struck with lead and flees, transforming into the laurel tree at the moment of capture — "a thin bark closed around her gentle bosom, and her hair became as moving leaves." Apollo claims the laurel as his sacred tree. The book closes with the seduction and persecution of [Io](../heroes/io.md) by Zeus.

- **Book II**: The story of [Phaethon](../heroes/phaethon.md) dominates the first half — his demand to drive the sun chariot to prove his parentage, Helius's reluctant consent sworn on the Styx, the uncontrolled flight that scorched the earth ("the Nile affrighted fled to parts remote"), and Zeus's thunderbolt strike that cast Phaethon into the river Eridanus. His sisters the Heliades weep for four months and are transformed into poplar trees weeping amber. Ocyroe, daughter of [Chiron](../creatures/chiron.md), is given prophetic speech and foretells [Asclepius](../deities/asclepius.md)'s destiny — to give health to the world, raise the dead, be struck by Zeus's bolt, and become a god — before being transformed into a mare for uttering what the fates forbade. [Hermes](../deities/hermes.md) slays Argus (who has a hundred eyes) with his wand while telling the story of [Pan](../deities/pan.md) and Syrinx, restoring [Io](../heroes/io.md) to human form. Callisto is transformed into a bear by [Artemis](../deities/artemis.md) after being seduced by [Zeus](../deities/zeus.md) (who disguised himself as Artemis herself); her son Arcas nearly kills her until Zeus places both as the constellations Ursa Major and Ursa Minor. [Apollo](../deities/apollo.md)'s love for Coronis, her betrayal, and the birth and abandonment of the infant [Asclepius](../deities/asclepius.md) on Coronis's funeral pyre round out the book.

- **Book III**: [Cadmus](../heroes/cadmus.md) founds Thebes — sent to find his sister Europa, he follows a cow to the site, kills the dragon of [Ares](../deities/ares.md), and sows its teeth to raise the Sparti (earth-born warriors who immediately slaughter each other). Actaeon stumbles on [Artemis](../deities/artemis.md) bathing and is transformed into a stag and torn apart by his own hounds. [Hera](../deities/hera.md) tricks Semele into demanding Zeus appear in his full divine form; Semele burns and the infant [Dionysus](../deities/dionysus.md) is rescued and sewn into Zeus's thigh ("an unformed babe was rescued from her side, and, nurtured in the thigh of Jupiter, completed Nature's time until his birth"). [Tiresias](../heroes/tiresias.md) is introduced: he ruled in favor of Zeus in the dispute with Hera over who experiences more pleasure, and Hera blinded him; Zeus compensated him with prophecy and seven lifespans. His first great prophecy concerns [Narcissus](../heroes/narcissus.md) — "if he but fail to recognize himself, a long life he may have" — confirmed when Narcissus wastes away gazing at his own reflection, Echo fading to voice and bone beside him, and both transformed: Narcissus into "a sweet flower, golden and white, the white around the gold." The book ends with Pentheus defying Dionysus, who causes his mother Agave and the other maenads to tear him to pieces.

- **Book IV**: A frame tale: Alcithoe and her sisters refuse the rites of [Dionysus](../deities/dionysus.md) and tell stories while weaving, until transformed into bats. Their tales include Pyramus and Thisbe — Babylonian lovers separated by a wall's crack, who arrange a tryst at the tomb of Ninus, and die by double suicide (the mulberry's dark fruit explained by Pyramus's blood soaking its roots). The sisters also tell the story of [Ares](../deities/ares.md) and [Aphrodite](../deities/aphrodite.md)'s adultery — [Helius](../deities/helius.md) reports it to [Hephaestus](../deities/hephaestus.md), who forges an invisible net and traps the lovers; the gods gather to laugh. [Perseus](../heroes/perseus.md) crosses Libya (where Medusa's dripping blood spawns countless serpents), turns [Atlas](../deities/atlas.md) to a mountain with the Gorgon's head, rescues [Andromeda](../heroes/andromeda.md) from the sea monster ("as marble he would deem her, but the breeze moved in her hair"), and defeats it by flying above and plunging his sword into its shoulder. Perseus recounts how he saw Medusa's reflection in his bronze shield and beheaded her — adding that Medusa was once beautiful, punished by [Athena](../deities/athena.md) for Poseidon's violation of her temple by having her hair changed to serpents.

- **Book V**: The wedding of Perseus and Andromeda is interrupted when Phineus, her former betrothed, attacks with an armed mob. Perseus fights with spear and sword until overwhelmed, then turns the Gorgon's head on his enemies, petrifying them one by one — including Phineus himself, who begged for mercy too late. Then the Muses tell the story of the nine [Pierides](../deities/groups/muses.md) who challenged them to a contest and were transformed into magpies. The main Muse narrative is the Rape of [Persephone](../deities/persephone.md): Cupid strikes [Hades](../deities/hades.md) with a golden arrow, Hades seizes the goddess at Henna in Sicily, [Demeter](../deities/demeter.md) searches the world with torches lit from Etna, blights the earth ("she broke with savage hand the plows which there had turned the soil"), transforms an insolent boy into a spotted lizard, and appeals to Zeus. Persephone is retrieved but has eaten seven pomegranate seeds — "the virgin, thoughtless while she strayed among the cultivated Stygian fields, had broken fast" — and must spend six months of each year underground. The nymph Arethusa tells how she was transformed into a spring by [Artemis](../deities/artemis.md) while fleeing the river god Alpheus, who still flows beneath the sea to mingle with her.

- **Book VI**: [Arachne](../heroes/arachne.md) of Lydia challenges [Athena](../deities/athena.md) to a weaving contest. Both produce technically flawless work: Athena depicts her contest with [Poseidon](../deities/poseidon.md) for Athens and cautionary scenes of mortals punished by gods; Arachne weaves the gods' seductions and deceptions — "Minerva could not find a fleck or flaw — even Envy can not censure perfect art." Enraged at the subject matter, Athena destroys the web and strikes Arachne, who hangs herself; Athena saves her but transforms her into a spider. [Niobe](../heroes/niobe.md), daughter of Tantalus and queen of Thebes, boasts that her seven sons and seven daughters surpass Leto's mere two children; [Apollo](../deities/apollo.md) kills all seven sons with arrows on the exercise field, [Artemis](../deities/artemis.md) kills all seven daughters, and Niobe is transformed into a weeping stone on Mount Sipylus. Marsyas is flayed by [Apollo](../deities/apollo.md) after losing a music contest; the satyr's blood and the tears of mourners become the river Marsyas. [Tereus](../heroes/tereus.md), king of Thrace, rapes his wife Procne's sister Philomela and cuts out her tongue; she weaves her story in cloth; Procne rescues her and kills their son Itys, serving his flesh to Tereus at dinner; all three are transformed into birds (Procne to swallow, Philomela to woodland bird, Tereus to hoopoe). Boreas abducts Orithyia, daughter of the Athenian king, and their children include Zetes and Calais.

- **Book VII**: [Jason](../heroes/jason.md) arrives in Colchis; [Medea](../heroes/medea.md)'s famous internal monologue — "I see a better course and I approve, but follow its defeat" — marks her surrender to love. She helps Jason sow the dragon's teeth and survive the earth-born warriors, and they sail for Iolcus with the Golden Fleece. Back home, Medea rejuvenates Jason's aged father Aeson with her herbs, boiling him in a cauldron with roots, stones, soil, dew, moonlight, and animal parts until he emerges dark-haired and vigorous. She tricks the daughters of Pelias into attempting the same for their father — but uses powerless herbs, killing him. She flees to Corinth, then Athens; when Jason takes a new wife, Medea burns the palace and kills her own sons. The book transitions to [Theseus](../heroes/theseus.md) and the Attic tales — Medea's failed poisoning attempt on Theseus, the Minotaur's bull connection, and the origin of the Myrmidons (Zeus transformed ants into men for Aeacus, who populated his island after a plague). The book closes with the tale of Cephalus and Procris — their mutual faithfulness tested and tragically ended.

- **Book VIII**: Scylla, daughter of King Nisus of Megara, betrays her city to King Minos of Crete for love of him; Minos refuses to take her and she is transformed into a bird (and her father, whose power lay in a purple hair she had cut, into the sea eagle that pursues her). The Minotaur is briefly mentioned; [Daedalus](../heroes/daedalus.md) builds the Labyrinth and then, imprisoned by Minos, engineers wings of feathers and wax for himself and his son Icarus. Icarus flies too high — "he saw the wax was melting" — falls into the sea, and Daedalus buries him. Perdix, Daedalus's nephew whom he had thrown from a height in jealousy, had been transformed by [Athena](../deities/athena.md) into a partridge that watched from a hedge. The Calydonian Boar hunt dominates the book's middle: [Artemis](../deities/artemis.md) sends a monstrous boar against Oeneus for neglecting her altars; heroes assemble including Atalanta, whose first arrow blooded the beast; Meleager kills it and awards the hide to Atalanta; his uncles dispute this; he kills them; his mother Althaea burns the fatal brand; Meleager dies as the brand is consumed. [Philemon](../heroes/philemon.md) and Baucis: the only couple who welcome [Zeus](../deities/zeus.md) and [Hermes](../deities/hermes.md) in disguise in Phrygia; their wine inexplicably replenishes; their cottage transforms into a marble temple; they ask to die together and become twin trees — lime and oak. [Erysichthon](../heroes/erysichthon.md) fells Demeter's sacred oak, kills a servant and a dryad nymph; Demeter sends Famine personified from Scythia; he is cursed with insatiable hunger, sells his daughter Mestra (who has shape-shifting power from Poseidon), and ultimately devours his own flesh.

- **Book IX**: [Heracles](../heroes/heracles.md) defeats the river god Achelous for [Deianira](../heroes/deianira.md)'s hand — Achelous describes the combat himself, losing a horn that became the cornucopia. Heracles kills the centaur Nessus on the river Evenus; Nessus gives Deianira his poisoned blood as a supposed love charm. The Hydra-poisoned shirt burns Heracles alive — "the poisoned garment, cleaving to him, ripped his skin, heat-shriveled, from his burning flesh" — but he lies placid on his pyre "as placid as if... you were safe, reclining on a banquet-couch." Zeus carries him to Olympus in his chariot and sets him among the stars ("his own celestial, wonderful appeared, majestic and of godlike dignity"). Ocyroe's earlier prophecy of Asclepius's destiny is fulfilled. The book then turns to Byblis, who falls incestuously in love with her twin brother Caunus, writes him an impassioned letter, is rejected, pursues him across the world, and dissolves in tears until transformed into a spring. Iphis, raised as a boy by her mother (to protect her from a father who wanted only sons), is betrothed to Ianthe; Isis transforms Iphis into a man in answer to her mother's prayers, and the marriage is celebrated.

- **Book X**: [Orpheus](../heroes/orpheus.md) descends to the underworld after Eurydice dies of a serpent's bite. His song moves even the tormented dead — "the anxious Tantalus stopped clutching at the return-flow of the wave, Ixion's twisting wheel stood wonder-bound." [Persephone](../deities/persephone.md) and [Hades](../deities/hades.md) grant Eurydice's release on the condition that Orpheus not look back; near the surface he turns, and she slips away calling only "Farewell!" He withdraws to Rhodope for three years, shunning women. Then, sitting on a hilltop, he sings and draws all the trees around him for shade; his songs form a nested anthology of myths including Ganymede (carried off to Olympus as cupbearer to Zeus), Hyacinthus (killed by a discus thrown by Apollo, transformed into a flower that bears the lament "AI AI"), [Pygmalion](../heroes/pygmalion.md) (the sculptor who loved his ivory statue; Aphrodite breathed life into it at her festival — "the ivory seemed to soften at the touch, and its firm texture yielded to his hand, as honey-wax of Mount Hymettus"), Myrrha (who tricked her father Cinyras into incest and was transformed into a myrrh tree, weeping amber tears), and [Adonis](../heroes/adonis.md) (born from Myrrha's tree, loved by Aphrodite who abandoned heaven for his company, warned against boars, killed by a boar's tusk, transformed into the anemone). Atalanta's footrace with Hippomenes is told as a cautionary tale by Aphrodite: the golden apples she provided won the race, but the couple forgot to thank her, and she turned them into lions.

- **Book XI**: The Ciconian maenads, enraged that Orpheus scorned their love, attack and kill him — his music held the stones at bay until their noise drowned his song. His severed head and lyre float down the Hebrus to Lesbos; [Apollo](../deities/apollo.md) prevents a serpent from biting them and turns it to stone. [Dionysus](../deities/dionysus.md) transforms the maenads into trees. In the underworld, Orpheus is reunited with Eurydice and can now look back at her "safely." [Midas](../heroes/midas.md) captures and feasts Silenus for ten days, returns him to Dionysus, and receives the gift of the golden touch — "cause whatsoever I shall touch to change at once to yellow gold." Food and wine turn to gold at his lips; he begs release; Dionysus sends him to wash in the Pactolus river, which thereafter runs with gold. Midas then retreats to the country, follows Pan, and when Pan challenges Apollo to a music contest on Mount Tmolus, Midas alone sides with Pan; Apollo gives him donkey ears ("still human, only this one part condemned"). A servant who cuts Midas's hair whispers the secret into a hole; reeds grow and repeat it in the wind. The next part concerns [Peleus](../heroes/peleus.md) and [Thetis](../deities/thetis.md): Peleus ambushes and wrestles the sea-goddess (who changes form repeatedly) and wins her. The tale of [Ceyx](../heroes/ceyx.md) and Alcyone occupies the book's end — Ceyx consults the oracle at Claros, is shipwrecked and drowned calling his wife's name; Hera sends Morpheus (son of Sleep) to appear to Alcyone as Ceyx's drowned form; she runs to the shore, sees his body, and transforms into a kingfisher mid-leap; the gods transform Ceyx too; their love persists in bird form; Aeolus calms the sea for seven halcyon days each winter so Alcyone can brood on her floating nest.

- **Book XII**: The Trojan War begins. At Aulis the fleet is becalmed; Achilles's father Cygnus (son of [Poseidon](../deities/poseidon.md)) has skin impervious to weapons; Achilles cannot pierce him with spear or sword, so strangles him with the helmet's thong — Cygnus is transformed into a white bird (fulfilling his name). During a feast on the truce's first night, the gathered heroes tell the story of Caeneus — formerly the girl Caenis who, after being violated by Poseidon, was granted any wish and chose to be an invulnerable man. The Battle of the Lapiths and Centaurs at Pirithous's wedding is narrated at length by Nestor: centaurs seized the bride and women, a massive battle followed, and Caeneus was finally buried alive under heaped tree trunks by the centaurs (invulnerable to weapons), emerging as a golden bird. [Achilles](../heroes/achilles.md) is killed — [Apollo](../deities/apollo.md) guides Paris's arrow to his one vulnerable point — and the Greeks mourn.

- **Book XIII**: [Ajax](../heroes/ajax.md) and [Odysseus](../heroes/odysseus.md) dispute the arms of the dead Achilles. Ajax lists his physical deeds; Odysseus speaks with eloquence — claiming descent from Zeus equal to Ajax's, detailing his recovery of Philoctetes and the Palladium, and arguing that the arms require a mind, not just strength: "the assembled body of the chiefs was moved; and then, appeared the power of eloquence: the fluent man received, amid applause, the arms of the brave man." Ajax kills himself from shame. After Troy falls, Achilles' ghost rises from the earth, "as large as when he lived," demanding the sacrifice of Polyxena; she dies with extraordinary dignity at the altar. [Hecuba](../heroes/hecuba.md) witnesses the worst of Troy's fall — Priam killed at Zeus's altar, Cassandra dragged away, Astyanax hurled from the tower — then finds her last son Polydorus murdered by the Thracian king Polymnestor for gold. She lures Polymnestor with a false promise of hidden treasure and gouges out his eyes; attacked by Thracians, she barks like a dog and is transformed. Memnon, son of [Eos](../deities/eos.md), is mourned by his mother; his ashes become the Memnonides birds. Then the nymph Galatea tells of the Cyclops Polyphemus's love for her and his crushing of her beloved Acis under a rock; Acis's blood becomes the river of that name. The sea-god Glaucus, a former fisherman transformed by a magical herb, falls in love with Scylla.

- **Book XIV**: Glaucus seeks [Circe](../deities/circe.md)'s help for Scylla's love; Circe herself wants Glaucus and, spurned, poisons Scylla's pool, transforming her into the monster of snapping dogs ("stiff bristles quickly grew out over me"). The [Sibyl of Cumae](../heroes/sibyl.md) tells [Aeneas](../heroes/aeneas.md) her story: [Apollo](../deities/apollo.md) offered eternal life for her virginity; she asked for years equal to grains of sand but forgot to ask for youth; now seven centuries old with three hundred more to come, she will eventually dwindle to nothing but a voice. Macareus, a surviving companion of Odysseus, tells a retelling of Circe's island — Circe is shown enthroned in purple and gold, surrounded by nymphs arranging herbs, "her fawning savage animals" at her feet; she transforms Odysseus's men to swine ("I felt my mouth take on a hardened skin with a long crooked snout") and restores them. [Aeneas](../heroes/aeneas.md)'s voyage to Italy and wars are briefly covered. [Circe](../deities/circe.md) transforms Picus (a Latin king) into a woodpecker for refusing her love; his wife Canens wastes away singing and dissolves into thin air. [Vertumnus](../deities/vertumnus.md), the Latin god of seasons and change, woos [Pomona](../heroes/pomona.md) through successive disguises — reaper, haymaker, ox-driver, soldier, fisherman, old woman — telling cautionary tales of divine wrath against those who reject love; when persuasion fails, he reveals his true divine form, blazing "as when the full light of the brilliant Sun... has shone forth with not one to intercept," and wins her love without force.

- **Book XV**: The philosophical centrepiece: Pythagoras delivers a long speech on transmigration of souls, the changing nature of all things, vegetarianism, and natural history. Ovid uses this to frame the transformation theme as a cosmic principle. [Asclepius](../deities/asclepius.md) is brought from Epidaurus to Rome: [Apollo](../deities/apollo.md) directs the Roman envoys to his son; Asclepius appears in the Roman leader's dream holding a staff and promising to come ("I will transform myself into this shape but of a greater size"); manifests as a golden serpent in his temple — "the god all glittering with gold and as a serpent, crest erect, sent forth a hissing" — boards the Roman ship, and travels along the Italian coast to Rome, settling on the Tiber island and curing the plague. Julius Caesar is deified — his soul ascending as a comet ("one flash of fire, that drew a lengthy trail") — fulfilling Augustus's inheritance. Ovid closes with his own claim to immortality through his poetry.

## Translation Notes

More's translation uses Roman/Latin divine names throughout:
- **Jove/Jupiter** = [Zeus](../deities/zeus.md); **Juno** = [Hera](../deities/hera.md); **Minerva** = [Athena](../deities/athena.md)
- **Venus** = [Aphrodite](../deities/aphrodite.md); **Mars** = [Ares](../deities/ares.md); **Mercury** = [Hermes](../deities/hermes.md)
- **Diana** = [Artemis](../deities/artemis.md); **Vulcan** = [Hephaestus](../deities/hephaestus.md); **Neptune** = [Poseidon](../deities/poseidon.md)
- **Pluto/Dis** = [Hades](../deities/hades.md); **Ceres** = [Demeter](../deities/demeter.md); **Bacchus/Liber** = [Dionysus](../deities/dionysus.md)
- **Phoebus/Apollo** = [Apollo](../deities/apollo.md); **Hercules** = [Heracles](../heroes/heracles.md); **Ulysses** = [Odysseus](../heroes/odysseus.md)
- **Sol** = [Helius](../deities/helius.md); **Aurora** = [Eos](../deities/eos.md); **Proserpine** = [Persephone](../deities/persephone.md)
- **Latona** = [Leto](../deities/leto.md)
